A brief explanation of ceramics terms.

I often find that people are interested in ceramics and pottery, but are confused by the terms used. So, here is a short list of common terms and methods.

  • High Fired - The temperature range of around 2250 to 2400 degrees Fahrenheit.
  • Low Fired - The wide temperature range of around 1400 to 2000 degrees Fahrenheit.
  • Cone - Potters and ceramists often say that something is fired to cone 10. The cone system is a measurement of both heat and time, and is a small cone that softens and bends at a chemically reliable point. This allows the person firing the kiln to see how much heat work has been done to the ware in the kiln. For more information, please see the Orton Ceramic website: https://www.ortonceramic.com/resources/product/pyrometric-cones/orton-cone-chart/
  • Reduction - The atmosphere of a kiln that is lacking the oxygen to completely burn. This causes the fuel to react with the oxygen in the glaze and clay.
  • Oxidation - The atmosphere of a kiln that has ample oxygen to burn the fuel. This is also typical of electric powered kilns. The ware in the kiln is also oxidized and is typically lighter in color than in reduction.
  • Porcelain - This type of clay is generally white to grey in color, and develops a strong tight body when fired. Natural porcelain is typically high fired.
  • Stoneware - This type of clay is generally tan to brown, with the color coming from the inclusion of natural metals and minerals. Most natural stoneware is high fired.
  • Kiln - Pronounced both "kill" and "kilnn", this is a structure that is able to withstand high temperatures for extended periods of time and is used to fire clay, turning it into ceramics.
  • Glaze - A glaze is often added to a ceramics piece for durability, aesthetics, or functionality. The glaze layer over the clay is often glassy and very strong.
  • Shino - A type of glaze that came from Japan, and is usually white to red. A highly varied glaze, the atmosphere is very important for how the color turns out. Shino glazes are high fired.
  • Celadon - A type of glaze that came from China, Korea, and Japan, and typically is a shade of green. There are also Celadons that are shades of blue, and ones that are clear or white. Most Celadons are high fired in reduction.
  • Raku - A style of firing that was developed in Japan for the tea ceremony. The ceramic ware is low fired, and historically under-fired to have a softer feel and sound. This generally means that Raku ware is not food safe by modern standards. The current American style of Raku is mostly decorative and uses a post-firing reduction to create lustrous metallic glazes and deep blacks.
  • Soda Fired - A type of firing where the ceramic ware is glazed by the introduction of baking soda or soda ash at high fire temperatures. This glazes the whole interior of the kiln with sodium, which is very reactive at high temperatures. The kiln must be specially prepared for soda firing, or the bricks will be eaten away by the sodium. This style of firing came from a search for an alternative to salt firing, which is very similar in methods, but has a different look and behavior.
  • Tenmoku or Temmoku - A glaze that is dark brown and black in most cases, named by Japanese Tea Masters for Tea Bowls (Chawan) from the Mountain of Heaven area in China. While most of my Tenmokus are fired in reduction, there are some Tenmoku glazes that only work in oxidation.
